6 CS FINAL DATA ANALYSIS ON 6 TRAIN STATIONS

By: Erion Dzemaili

About the Project

A detailed data analysis of the neighborhoods surrounding NYC 6 Train stations, including graphs that compare economic and geographic relationships.

Data Spreadsheet (Income & Population — Rounded)

Walk Score vs Population

Analysis: The scatterplot shows that stations with lower Walk Scores (mainly those above 100th Street and in the Bronx) often serve larger nearby populations. These neighborhoods are more residential with fewer commercial corridors, producing Walk Scores in the 58–85 range but populations often above 80,000. In contrast, stations in Manhattan — especially below 59th Street — have extremely high Walk Scores (97–100) but smaller local populations (45,000–60,000), reflecting dense commercial land use and campus zones rather than residential blocks.

Conclusion: There is no strong positive correlation between Walk Score and population size. Walkable areas are not necessarily more populated; differences appear driven more by land use (commercial vs. residential development).

Walk Score vs Median Income

Analysis: Stations with Walk Scores above 95 — primarily in Midtown, Downtown Manhattan, and the Lower East Side — show very high median incomes ($110,000–$160,000). Walkability in these districts corresponds with high-income residents and dense commercial infrastructure. By contrast, Bronx stations with Walk Scores between 58 and 88 have much lower median incomes ($42,000–$55,000). These neighborhoods tend to have wider roadways, fewer businesses, and more vehicle-dependent infrastructure.

Conclusion: There is a moderate positive correlation between Walk Score and median income. Wealthier neighborhoods tend to be more walkable, likely reflecting investments in urban design, public transit, and commercial density.

ZIP Code vs Walk Score

Analysis: Manhattan ZIP codes (100xx) consistently show Walk Scores between 97 and 100, reflecting extremely walkable environments across both residential and commercial areas. Bronx ZIP codes (104xx) vary widely from 58 to 96, indicating a more mixed urban layout with differences in density, retail access, and street design.

Conclusion: ZIP code alone does not strongly predict Walk Score, but it highlights a clear borough-level divide: Manhattan is uniformly walkable, while the Bronx varies significantly by neighborhood.
