6 CS FINAL DATA ANALYSIS ON 6 TRAIN STATIONS

By: Erion Dzemaili

About the Project

A detailed data analysis of the neighborhoods surrounding NYC 6 Train stations, including graphs that compare economic and geographic relationships.

Data Spreadsheet (Income & Population — Rounded)

Walk Score vs Nicche

Analysis: Neighborhoods with strong Niche rankings consistently have very high Walk Scores (typically above 95). Lower-ranked neighborhoods show more variation in walkability, though most still remain relatively walkable due to New York City’s dense urban layout.

Conclusion: There is a clear association between walkability and neighborhood ranking. Neighborhoods that are easier to navigate on foot tend to score better on Niche’s livability scale, highlighting the importance of pedestrian-friendly infrastructure in perceived quality of life.

Median Income VS Niche

Analysis: Neighborhoods with better Niche rankings (lower numerical values) show substantially higher median incomes. Top-ranked Manhattan neighborhoods consistently fall in the $120,000–$160,000 income range, while lower-ranked Bronx neighborhoods cluster below $50,000.

Conclusion: There is a strong relationship between median income and Niche ranking. Higher-income neighborhoods tend to receive better livability rankings, indicating that economic factors play a major role in how neighborhood quality is evaluated.

Population vs Niche

Analysis: This scatterplot compares neighborhood population size with Niche’s ranking of the best neighborhoods to live in New York City. Lower-ranked (better-ranked) neighborhoods cluster around smaller populations, primarily in Manhattan. Higher-ranked (worse-ranked) neighborhoods, mostly in the Bronx, tend to serve significantly larger populations.

Conclusion: There is no strong correlation between population size and neighborhood quality as measured by Niche. Highly ranked neighborhoods are not necessarily more populated; instead, larger populations are often associated with lower-ranked neighborhoods, suggesting that density alone does not determine perceived livability.
